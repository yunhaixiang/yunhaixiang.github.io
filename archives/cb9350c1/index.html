Musings on Education

Dec 7 2023 7 minutes read (About 1040 words)

“Teaching is not about information. It’s about having an honest intellectual relationship with your students.”

Paul Lockhart

It’s been 14 years since the publication of Paul Lockhart’s celebrated Lament, in which he envisioned a pedagogy of mathematics that emphasizes aesthetics, inquiry, and intuition. Lockhart’s critical insights resonated with me profoundly, however, after more than a decade, his vision has not materialized. In this post, I wish to delineate more concretely what I have in mind a curriculum in his spirit, which includes mathematics as well as other subjects, would look like if it were to be implemented.

Two Cultures of Pedagogy

Before we get into the topic, it is important to examine the status quo of (secondary) math education in the world. There are primarily two styles: the western style popular in first world western countries, and eastern style examplified by countries like China, India, Japan, Korea, etc. As a person with mathematical education from both the east and the west, I noticed the polarization of the styles of pedagogy between the two cultures. They arose as a result of complicated sociological and cultural reasons, which I don’t intend to discuss. What I want to know is the merits and demerits of each. From my own experience, I highlight these defining differences (only considering secondary level education): the eastern style emphasizes much more on practice and repetition; the western style has more advanced topics such as calculus and linear algebra; the eastern style has more practice on proofs and more much difficult exam and practice problems; the western style attempts to provide some degree of inquiry-based learning but not very successfully. Both styles, however, are formulaic in exams and practice problems. Since the typical eastern-style educated student outperforms their western counterparts in standarized exams, it seems prima facie that the eastern-style education is superior. This is not true, as the eastern style education demands high hours of learning and provides more unpleasant learning experience. This does a disservice to the students who would have been interested in higher level mathematics, but walked away from it having the wrong idea of what the discipline of math is about or believing erroneously that they are unsuitable for it. I won’t regurgitate more criticisms made by Lockhart here. The question remains is how to change them. I believe to tackle this, we must first identify the kinds of students there are.

The Artist, the Philosopher, and the Engineer

One of the most rediculous cliches is the notion of a math person versus a non-math person, as if some people are born suitable for math and some not. This is reinforced by the unfortunate status quo of our education. I contend that we should not classify students according to how they perform in different subjects, but what motivates each students internally. I summerize three common kinds of people that I observe according to their academic motives, which I call: the artist, the philosopher, and the engineer. These are not meant to be taken literally as people who are interested in art, philosophy, or engineering, but three archetypical academic personality types.

  • The motive of the artist is to dream, feel, and express;
  • The motive of the philosopher is to discover, analyze, and understand;
  • The motive of the engineer is to invent, design, and craft;

These are, of course, not mutually exclusive – in fact most people and most subjects have different degrees of several. Indeed, in some ways mathematics is like art, in some ways philosophy, and in some ways engineering. I don’t claim that the list is exhausive either, but I identify them as the most prominent.

I claim that a good pedagogy is one that encourages students to indulge in their academic motives, not one that crams information or bores students with needless repetition. For students who belong to the philosophers, this is what is traditionally known as inquiry-based learning; for the engineers, this is known as action learning; and so on. In this ethos, I also classify the traditional academic subjects in three categories.

  • Creative and Artistic: Literary Arts, Visual Arts, Performing Arts
  • Analytical and Scientific: Math, Physics, Computer Science, Chemistry, Biology, Psychology, Philosophy
  • Social and Cultural: History, Economics, Politics, Law, Linguistics, Foreign Languages, Sociology

These do not correspond one to one to the kinds of students, but they are roughly based on commonality of interests, nor is this an exhausive list either. Unrelated, but I’ve always wondered why it is common to teach literature and literary arts in high schools, but not for visual arts and performing arts. It never made sense to me.

Problem Solving vs. Theory Building

There are generally two cultures of math: the problems solvers and the theory builders. Our current math curriculum, whether east or west, has a significant lack of emphasis on theory building. Some curriculums don’t touch on advanced theories such as calculus and linear algebra at all; some do, but only superficially and computationally. I’ve always believed a good high school mathematical curriculum must have proofs, and must have materials on more advanced theories. I contend that it is completely possible to teach the average motivated high school student elementary number theory, combinatorics, abstract algebra, and rigorous calculus. In fact, these are the topics a motivated student probably finds more interesting. I’m disheartened to see that many students are drowning in repetitive problems in less interesting topics, and resenting math for it.

Inquiry-based Standardized Exams

Standardized exams are boring, but nevertheless it is necessary for practical purposes. So the question remains is how to make them interesting. I contend that we should make exams themselves teach student new outside-curriculum materials. For example, an exam could be designed as such: the student reads an expository article of a new math topic that they have not learned about, then the student is asked to solve related problems of it using what he learned during and before the exam. The article should provide example proofs the student could imitate and elaborate on, and guide the student step by step. This also makes preparing for exams and practicing with past exams less boring and more intellectually stimulating.
